# Script

Converting units seems straightforward, and that's because it is; but, we've got a visual method that will save your butt. Let's say I wanted to convert 14.2 miles to kilometers, and one kilometer = 0.62 miles. Most people would do this: $$ 14.2 \text{ miles} \times 0.62 = 8.804 \text{ kilometers}$$
But that's wrong. 14.2 miles is actually 22.9 kilometers, which I got from *dividing* 0.62, not multiplying. But you won't *always* divide to convert units, sometimes you'll multiply, like this: $$ 3.2 \text{ meters} \times 1000 = 3200 \text{ centimeters} $$
...which is correct. That's frustrating, I wish there was a more intuitive way of converting units! And that is a segue to me saying: yes, there is. Let me show you.

Take a look at our example with miles and kilometers. We know that one kilometer = 0.62 miles. Okay, let's imagine that as a fraction: $$\frac{1 \text{ kilometer}}{0.62 \text{ miles}}$$
Now watch this setup: I can multiply any distance in miles by this fraction, and the "miles" will cancel out. Also, I can see what numbers get multiplied: the ones in the top part of the fraction, while the numbers in the bottom part of the fraction get divided! $$ 14.2 \cancel{ miles} \times \frac{1 \text{ kilometer}} {0.62 \cancel{ miles}}$$
So, since $0.62$ is in the bottom part of the fraction, we can visually see that have to divide!
$$ 14.2 \cancel{ miles} \times \frac{1 \text{ kilometer}} {0.62 \cancel{ miles}} = 14.2 \div 0.62 = 22.9 \text{ kilometers}$$

Note that whenever you do this, all you gotta do is multiply all the number on the top, and divide all the numbers on the bottom. ![[unitConversion_1.png]]

If I wanted to multiply from kilometers to miles, I could flip the fraction over: $$\frac{1 \text{ kilometer}}{0.62 \text{ miles}} \rightarrow \frac{0.62 \text{ miles}}{1 \text{ kilometer}}$$
So now, I could figure out what 35.6 kilometers is in miles. $$ 35.6 \cancel{ kilometers} \times \frac{0.62 \text{ miles}}{1 \cancel{ kilometer}} = 22.08 \text{ miles}$$ 
Now, I know what you're thinking: isn't this a lot of work just to do one conversion? And it is... but what if we did a lot of conversions at once? Take a look.

Say I wanted to convert 35 miles into meters, and I knew that 0.62 miles = 1 kilometer and that 1000 meters = 1 kilometer. I can turn those into two fractions: $$ \frac{1 \text{ kilometer}}{0.62 \text{ miles}} \space \frac{1000 \text{ meters}}{1 \text{ kilometer}}$$
And then, I can set them up into one long conversion like this: $$ 35 \cancel{ miles} \times \frac{1 \cancel{ kilometer}}{0.62 \cancel{ miles}} \times \frac{1000 \text{ meters}}{1 \cancel{ kilometer}}$$
Notice how I canceled out "miles" because it's both on the top AND the bottom of the set-up, same thing with "kilometers". The only unit that shouldn't be cancelled out is our "goal" unit, the one we want to convert *into*, which is meters!
